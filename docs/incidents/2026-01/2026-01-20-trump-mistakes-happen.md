# Trump on ICE Violence: "Mistakes Happen" While Focusing on "Murders and Criminals"

## Summary
At a White House briefing marking one year in office, President Trump acknowledged ICE agents "make mistakes sometimes" while defending enforcement operations. Like DHS Secretary Noem, Trump redirected attention to criminal deportees, flipping through pages of arrest records and stating agents are "apprehending murderers and drug dealers." The statement drew criticism for dismissing documented civil rights abuses as isolated errors when evidence suggests a systemic pattern of racial profiling and excessive force.

## Official Statement

### Trump on ICE "Mistakes"
At the White House briefing on January 20, 2026, Trump stated:

> "They're going to make mistakes sometimes. ICE is going to be too rough with somebody, or, you know, they deal with rough people. They're going to make a mistake. Sometimes it can happen. We feel terribly."

Referring to the fatal shooting of Renee Good in Minneapolis, Trump said he "felt horribly" when he heard about her death, calling it "a tragedy" and "a horrible thing."

### Redirecting to "Murderers and Drug Dealers"
Trump's solution to criticism was to display criminal histories of those apprehended during Minnesota operations. Standing at the briefing room podium, he flipped through pages of arrest records and stated:

> "They don't say it like they should. They're apprehending murderers and drug dealers, a lot of bad people."

On social media, Trump emphasized:

> "The Department of Homeland Security and ICE must start talking about the murderers and other criminals."

Trump lauded ICE for allegedly arresting "tens of thousands of illegal alien gang members, drug dealers, murderers, drug runners, human traffickers, fraudsters, and savage criminals."

## Analysis

### The "Criminals and Murderers" Deflection Pattern
Trump's rhetoric mirrors DHS Secretary Noem's consistent messaging, which emphasizes "the worst of the worst criminal illegal aliens, including pedophiles, murderers, gang members, terrorists, and rapists." This framing serves to justify enforcement operations and deflect from documented civil rights concerns.

However, the data contradicts this focus:
- **74% of nearly 70,000 immigrants in detention have no criminal convictions** (ICE detention statistics, December 2025)
- According to CNN internal ICE statistics, **less than 10% of immigrants in ICE custody since October 2024 had criminal offenses**
- The Cato Institute reported **65% of ICE detainees had no criminal convictions, and 93% had no violent convictions**

### Why "Mistakes Happen" Is Inadequate

The "mistakes happen" framing fails to account for documented systemic patterns:

**1. Legal Protests and Recording Treated as Criminal Acts**
The ACLU's class-action lawsuit (*Hussen v. Noem*) documents suspicionless stops and warrantless arrests based on perceived ethnicity. U.S. citizens have been detained for filming operations, exercising First Amendment rights.

**2. Systemic Use of Citizen Checks**
Multiple documented incidents show U.S. citizens stopped and questioned based on appearance, accent, or proximity to enforcement operations—not isolated "mistakes" but a pattern of racial profiling.

**3. Predictable Violence, Not Accidents**
Former DOJ Civil Rights Division attorneys wrote that Renee Good's death was "a predictable one," citing "countless videos of ICE officers using force that reflected poor training, a refusal to de-escalate and a growing corps of masked agents acting with brutal impunity." They characterized the shooting as "textbook excessive force" rather than a regrettable mistake.

**4. Pattern of Deporting Lawful Residents**
The deportation of individuals with legal status—including Kilmar Abrego García (had withholding of removal protection) and Any Lucia Lopez Belloza (legal student)—suggests enforcement beyond "criminals." As one analyst noted: "These were legal pathways. People did the thing the government asked them to do, and this government went and preemptively revoked that status."

### Criticism from Civil Rights Organizations

**Human Rights Watch** documented 13 harmful policies in its first-year review, noting that ICE detention reached 68,400 people in December 2025—a record high—with mandatory detention policies eliminating bond hearings for many immigrants.

**The NAACP and coalition partners** wrote to Congress warning against granting DHS a "blank check," citing "record-high deaths in custody, wrongful detention of U.S. citizens, lack of transparency, expansion of dragnet surveillance, and the misuse of military resources in civilian immigration enforcement."

**National Urban League President Mark Morial** characterized the situation as a "state of emergency," stating the country "is creeping toward totalitarianism."

### What the "Mistakes" Framing Ignores

The characterization of civil rights violations as "mistakes" fails to address:
- Why DHS policy explicitly authorizes "collateral" arrests of individuals encountered coincidentally
- Why enforcement operations continue in the same manner after documented violations
- Why DOJ declined to investigate the Renee Good shooting despite resignations by federal prosecutors
- Why 74% of detainees have no criminal record if operations target "the worst of the worst"
